    Chapter 3

    The Road to Independence

    After 1763, taxes, military presence, and arguments over representation pushed many colonists toward independence.

    Understanding is stronger than memorization. This chapter connects facts to meaning and real civic life.

    Learning Objectives

    • Explain why tensions grew with Britain
    • Understand “No Taxation Without Representation”
    • Describe the events leading to independence

    After the French and Indian War

    Britain won the war in 1763 but had large debts. Parliament decided the colonies should help pay for imperial defense, creating new taxes such as the Sugar Act and Stamp Act.

    No Taxation Without Representation

    Colonists argued that Parliament should not tax them because they had no elected representatives in Parliament. The issue was not simply money; it was political representation and consent.

    Protest and Escalation

    Events such as the Boston Massacre, Tea Act, Boston Tea Party, and Intolerable Acts deepened distrust. The colonies began cooperating through the Continental Congress.

    Toward War

    In April 1775, fighting at Lexington and Concord began the Revolutionary War. By July 4, 1776, the Continental Congress adopted the Declaration of Independence.

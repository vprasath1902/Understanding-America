## More Than the President

When people picture the executive branch, they think of the President — but the President leads an enormous organization. The branch includes the Vice President, the Cabinet, fifteen executive **departments**, dozens of independent **agencies**, the armed forces, and roughly two million civilian federal employees. Its job, defined by the Constitution, is to **carry out** — execute — the laws that Congress passes. Writing a law is one thing; turning it into real programs, services, and enforcement is the executive branch's work.

## Departments and Agencies

The work is divided among specialists. **Cabinet departments** such as State, Treasury, Defense, Justice, and Homeland Security each handle a broad area of national life. Within and alongside them are **agencies** that focus on specific tasks. To do their jobs, agencies write **regulations** — detailed rules that fill in how a law actually works — within the authority Congress grants them. This large system of departments and agencies is sometimes called the federal **bureaucracy**.

## Everyday Examples

The executive branch touches ordinary life constantly, often without notice. USCIS processes citizenship and green cards; the Department of State issues passports; the IRS collects federal taxes and sends refunds; the TSA screens airline passengers; the Social Security Administration sends retirement and disability benefits; and the National Park Service manages the national parks. Nearly every interaction a person has with the federal government runs through an executive agency.

## Career Public Service

Most of the people doing this work are not politicians. They are **civil service** professionals — scientists, inspectors, clerks, analysts, and specialists — hired for their expertise and expected to serve the public fairly regardless of which party controls the White House. A new President appoints the top leaders, but the career workforce provides continuity, so the government keeps running smoothly through every change in administration.

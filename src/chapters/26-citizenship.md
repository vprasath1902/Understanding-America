## The Naturalization Path

For most people, becoming a citizen by **naturalization** follows a clear path. An applicant must first meet the eligibility requirements — usually holding a green card as a **permanent resident** for a set number of years, showing good moral character, and meeting residence and physical-presence rules. The applicant then files **Form N-400**, the Application for Naturalization, pays the fee (see Appendix H for current amounts), and attends a **biometrics** appointment where USCIS records fingerprints and a photo. After that comes the interview, and finally the oath.

## The Interview and Tests

At the interview, a USCIS officer reviews the N-400 line by line, confirms the applicant's identity, and asks questions to verify eligibility. The officer also administers two tests unless the applicant qualifies for an exemption: an **English test** (speaking, reading, and writing) and a **civics test** about U.S. history and government. The civics test draws on 100 official questions; the officer asks up to ten, and answering six correctly passes. The next chapters of this book walk through that civics material.

## The Oath of Allegiance

Passing the tests and meeting all requirements leads to approval — but a person is not yet a citizen. Citizenship becomes official only at the **Oath of Allegiance** ceremony. In the oath, new citizens make several promises: to **give up loyalty to other countries**, to **support and defend the Constitution** and laws of the United States, to **obey the laws**, and to **serve the nation** (including in the military or other important work) if needed. At that moment, they receive a **Certificate of Naturalization** and become full citizens. Many ceremonies also include the **Pledge of Allegiance**, in which Americans show loyalty to the United States and the flag.

## Rights of Citizens

Citizenship opens doors that are closed to non-citizens: the right to **vote** in federal elections, to **run for federal office**, to **serve on a jury**, to carry a U.S. **passport**, and to petition for certain family members to immigrate. (Some rights — like freedom of speech, religion, and assembly — belong to *everyone* living in the United States, citizen or not.) Four constitutional amendments protect the right to vote: the 15th (race), the 19th (women), the 24th (no poll tax), and the 26th (age 18).

## Responsibilities of Citizens

With those rights come responsibilities. Citizens are expected to **obey the law**, **pay federal taxes** (required by law and by the 16th Amendment), **serve on a jury** when called, and **vote** in elections. All **men ages 18 through 25** — citizens and many non-citizens alike — must register for the **Selective Service**, so a military draft could be organized fairly if it were ever needed. Beyond these duties, citizens can take part through **civic participation**: joining a community or political group, contacting elected officials, or running for office. Citizenship is best understood not as an ending but as the beginning of a lifelong, active relationship with the country.

This chapter and the three that follow walk through the civics knowledge the USCIS test covers. You have already met most of these ideas earlier in the book; here they are gathered as the test frames them, so you can study the facts and understand the meaning behind them.

## The Constitution Is the Supreme Law

The **Constitution is the supreme law of the land** — no law or official may override it. It does three things at once: it sets up the government, defines and limits the government's powers, and protects the basic rights of the people. When the test asks "What is the supreme law of the land?", the answer is the Constitution.

## We the People

The Constitution opens with the words "**We the People**." Those first three words capture a revolutionary idea: government receives its authority from the people themselves, not from a king. This principle is called **self-government** (or popular sovereignty), and it is why citizens vote, serve on juries, and can hold office.

## The Bill of Rights

The first **ten amendments** to the Constitution are the **Bill of Rights**. They protect freedoms that government may not take away, including freedom of **speech, religion, the press, assembly, and petition** (all in the First Amendment), as well as protections for fair treatment by the legal system. When the test asks how many amendments are in the Bill of Rights, the answer is ten.

## The Declaration of Independence

The **Declaration of Independence**, adopted on July 4, 1776, announced that the American colonies were free and independent from Great Britain. It declared that people have certain **inalienable rights** — among them "life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness" — and that governments exist to protect those rights. Thomas Jefferson was its main author.

## Fear of Another King

Having just fought a war against a distant, all-powerful monarch, Americans were determined not to create a new one. Their first plan of government, the **Articles of Confederation**, reflected that fear: it kept most power in the thirteen states and created only a weak national Congress. There was no President to enforce laws and no national court system. The states behaved almost like thirteen small countries loosely tied together — a **confederation**.

## Weaknesses of the Articles

The weaknesses soon became impossible to ignore. The national Congress **could not tax**, so it could not pay its debts or reliably fund an army; it could only ask the states for money and hope they paid. It **could not regulate trade** between the states, so they raised barriers against one another. It could not settle disputes between states effectively, and changing the Articles required the agreement of all thirteen. The government was nearly powerless to act.

## Shays' Rebellion

The crisis came to a head in 1786–1787, when **Shays' Rebellion** — an uprising of debt-burdened farmers in Massachusetts — challenged state authority. The national government had no power to help put it down. The event alarmed leaders such as **George Washington** and **James Madison**, who feared the young republic might dissolve into disorder without a stronger national government.

## The Philadelphia Convention

In 1787, delegates gathered in Philadelphia at the **Constitutional Convention**, officially to revise the Articles. They quickly concluded that patching the old system was not enough and instead wrote an entirely new **Constitution**. Their central challenge was to design a government **strong enough to govern but limited enough to protect liberty** — and meeting it required hard **compromises** among large and small states. How they solved that problem is the subject of the next chapter.

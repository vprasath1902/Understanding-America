## Shared Power

The United States is not a single, all-powerful government. It is a **federal** system: one national government in Washington, D.C., shares power with fifty state governments, and each state in turn creates the local governments — counties, cities, and townships — within it. This arrangement is called **federalism**. The founders chose it as another way to limit power, dividing authority not only among three branches but also between the national and state levels.

## Federal Responsibilities

The Constitution gives the national government a defined set of powers — its **enumerated powers**. These are matters that must be handled the same way for the whole country: immigration and citizenship, national defense and foreign policy, printing and coining money, issuing passports, regulating trade between states and with other nations, and collecting federal taxes. Only the federal government can do these things.

## State and Local Responsibilities

Powers not given to the federal government are **reserved** to the states under the **Tenth Amendment**. States run most of the matters people deal with day to day: schools, driver licenses, state roads and police, marriage and birth records, zoning, and local courts. States then delegate many services to local governments. Some powers are **concurrent** — shared by both levels — such as the power to tax, build roads, and operate courts.

## Conflict and Supremacy

Because two levels of government make laws, they sometimes conflict. The **Supremacy Clause** of the Constitution settles the question: when a valid federal law and a state law genuinely conflict, federal law prevails. But the federal government cannot simply override the states on matters reserved to them. When it is unclear whether a real conflict exists — or whether the federal government has overstepped — the courts decide. This balance between national unity and state independence is still debated today.

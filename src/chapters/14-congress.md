## Article I Comes First

The Constitution describes Congress in **Article I**, before the President or the courts. That order was deliberate: the founders expected lawmaking by elected representatives to be the central work of a republic. Congress is the branch closest to the people, and it holds the power that touches daily life most directly — the power to write laws, to tax, and to spend.

## Two Chambers — the Great Compromise

Congress is **bicameral**, meaning it has two chambers. This design settled a fierce argument at the Constitutional Convention. Large states wanted representation based on population; small states wanted every state treated equally. The **Great Compromise** gave each side one chamber: the **House of Representatives**, where seats are divided by population, and the **Senate**, where every state has two senators regardless of size. A bill must pass both chambers, so laws need the support of both the people and the states.

## The Powers of Congress

Congress holds broad authority. It makes federal laws, levies taxes, and decides how federal money is spent through **appropriations**. It can declare war, raise and fund the armed forces, regulate commerce between the states, and coin money. It also provides **oversight** of the executive branch — holding hearings, investigating problems, and confirming or rejecting officials (in the Senate). These powers let Congress shape national policy and hold the other branches accountable.

## How the Work Gets Done

With hundreds of members and thousands of bills, Congress relies on **committees** to do the detailed work. Committees specialize in areas such as the budget, defense, or the judiciary; they study proposed bills, hold hearings where experts and citizens testify, amend the text, and decide which bills move forward. Most bills never make it out of committee. Understanding committees is the key to understanding how an idea actually becomes a law — the subject of a later chapter.

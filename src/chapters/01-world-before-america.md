## Before There Was a United States

The land that became the United States has a deep past. For thousands of years before any European arrived, **Indigenous peoples** lived across North America in hundreds of distinct nations — among them the Cherokee, the Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy, the Navajo, and the Powhatan. They had their own languages, religions, economies, and systems of government, some quite sophisticated. The Haudenosaunee Confederacy, for example, joined several nations under a shared council. America's story begins here, not in 1776.

## Why Europeans Came

Beginning in the late 1400s, European powers crossed the Atlantic seeking **land, trade, wealth, religious freedom, and national advantage**. Spain, France, England, and the Netherlands all established footholds. Their arrival brought lasting and often devastating change for Indigenous peoples, including disease, displacement, and conflict, even as it began to reshape the continent.

## England and the Atlantic Colonies

England eventually planted a string of **colonies** along the Atlantic coast — from the Jamestown settlement in Virginia (1607) to the Pilgrims at Plymouth (1620) and beyond. Distance from London meant colonists often governed themselves through **colonial assemblies**, town meetings, and local courts. These everyday experiences of electing representatives and making local laws built habits of **self-government** that would prove decisive later.

## Why This Matters

The American Revolution did not erupt out of nowhere or in an empty land. It grew from centuries of Indigenous history, European colonization, and a growing colonial tradition of governing themselves. When colonists later objected that Britain was ignoring their right to consent, they were defending a practice they had lived for generations. Understanding this background makes the founding of the United States far easier to understand.

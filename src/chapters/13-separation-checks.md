## Why Divide Power

The founders had just fought a war against a king, and they feared putting too much power in any one set of hands. Their solution was to divide the federal government into three branches, each with its own role: a **legislative** branch to make laws, an **executive** branch to carry them out, and a **judicial** branch to interpret them. This division is called the **separation of powers**, and the idea behind it is simple — liberty is safer when no single institution can do everything.

## The Three Branches

Congress (the legislative branch) writes the laws and controls federal spending. The President (the executive branch) enforces the laws, leads the military, and conducts relations with other countries. The courts (the judicial branch) settle disputes and decide what the laws and the Constitution mean. Each branch has its own source of authority in the Constitution, and each is filled in a different way — Congress and the President by election, judges by appointment.

## Checks and Balances

Separation alone is not enough, so the Constitution also gives each branch ways to limit the others — **checks and balances**. The President can **veto** a bill, but Congress can **override** the veto with a two-thirds vote in each chamber. The President appoints judges and top officials, but the Senate must confirm them, and the Senate must approve treaties. Congress controls the budget and can **impeach** and remove a President or judge for serious misconduct. The courts can use **judicial review** to strike down laws or executive actions that violate the Constitution. No branch can act entirely on its own.

## No One Is Above the Constitution

The deepest principle here is that the Constitution, not any official, holds the highest authority. Presidents, members of Congress, and judges all take an oath to support the Constitution. When leaders disagree about the limits of their power, the dispute is settled through this constitutional system rather than by force — a peaceful design that has held for more than two centuries.

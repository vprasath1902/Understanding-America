## What Is the Cabinet?

The Cabinet is a group of the President's senior advisors. Each Cabinet member leads one major department of the federal government — think of them as the heads of the largest government organizations. The Cabinet is part of the Executive Branch; it is **not** a separate branch of government.

## Why the President Needs a Cabinet

No single person can manage the military, federal taxes, immigration, education, transportation, agriculture, public health, and diplomacy all at once. The Cabinet lets the President delegate these responsibilities to experienced leaders who each run one department — much like a chief executive who relies on the leaders of finance, engineering, and operations instead of doing every job personally.

## How Cabinet Members Are Chosen

The President nominates each Cabinet member. For most Cabinet positions, the U.S. Senate must confirm the nomination before the person can serve. This is another example of checks and balances: the President cannot fill these offices alone.

## Who Cabinet Members Report To

Cabinet members report to the President, not to Congress. Congress still provides oversight — it approves department budgets, holds hearings, requests testimony, investigates department actions, and passes the laws that departments must follow. Congress oversees the departments, but it does not manage them.

## The 15 Executive Departments

| Department | Leader | Main Responsibility |
| --- | --- | --- |
| State | Secretary of State | Foreign policy and diplomacy |
| Treasury | Secretary of the Treasury | Taxes, federal finances, and currency |
| Defense | Secretary of Defense | Military and national defense |
| Justice | Attorney General | Federal law enforcement and legal matters |
| Interior | Secretary of the Interior | Public lands and natural resources |
| Agriculture | Secretary of Agriculture | Farming and food programs |
| Commerce | Secretary of Commerce | Business and economic development |
| Labor | Secretary of Labor | Workers, employment, and labor laws |
| Health and Human Services | Secretary of HHS | Public health and healthcare programs |
| Housing and Urban Development | Secretary of HUD | Housing and community development |
| Transportation | Secretary of Transportation | Roads, aviation, rail, and transit safety |
| Energy | Secretary of Energy | Energy policy and nuclear security |
| Education | Secretary of Education | Federal education policy |
| Veterans Affairs | Secretary of Veterans Affairs | Services for military veterans |
| Homeland Security | Secretary of Homeland Security | Border security, immigration, cybersecurity, and disaster response |

## Departments, Agencies, and USCIS

Many well-known agencies belong to a Cabinet department. For example, the Department of Homeland Security includes the TSA, FEMA, the Secret Service, Customs and Border Protection (CBP), Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE), and U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS). So USCIS — which handles naturalization and green cards — is part of the Department of Homeland Security.

## How a Department Carries Out a Law

When Congress passes a law and the President signs it, an executive department puts it into action. Suppose Congress creates a new veterans' benefit: Congress writes and passes the law, the President signs it, the Department of Veterans Affairs builds the programs and administers the benefit, and the federal courts resolve any disputes about how the law is applied. This shows how all three branches interact.

## The States' Voice

The Senate balances the House. Where House seats follow population, the Senate gives **every state two senators**, no matter how large or small — 100 senators in all. This **equal representation** was part of the Great Compromise, and it means a senator from a small state has the same vote as one from the largest. The design ensures that national decisions need broad support across states, not just in the most populous regions.

## Six-Year, Staggered Terms

Senators serve **six-year terms**, three times as long as a House member's. The terms are also **staggered**: the Senate is divided into three groups, and only about **one-third** of seats are up for election every two years. As a result, the Senate is never entirely new — experienced members always remain. This gives the chamber stability and continuity, encouraging it to take a longer view than the fast-changing House. Senators must be at least 30 years old, U.S. citizens for nine years, and live in the state they represent.

## Unique Powers

The Constitution gives the Senate several powers the House does not have. The Senate **confirms** major presidential appointments — Cabinet secretaries, ambassadors, and federal judges, including Supreme Court justices. It **approves treaties** with other nations by a two-thirds vote. And when the House impeaches an official, the Senate holds the **impeachment trial** and decides whether to remove the person from office. These powers make the Senate a powerful check on the President.

## Direct Election

The Senate was not always elected by the people. Originally, **state legislatures** chose each state's senators. The **Seventeenth Amendment**, ratified in 1913, changed that, giving the choice directly to voters. Today you elect your senators yourself — another example of how the Constitution has expanded democratic participation over time.

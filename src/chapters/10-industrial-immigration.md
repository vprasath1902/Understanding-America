## An Industrial Power

After the Civil War, the United States grew into an industrial giant. The transcontinental railroad, completed in 1869, linked the country coast to coast. Steel, oil, and manufacturing built enormous fortunes and powerful companies. Cities grew quickly as people moved from farms to take factory jobs, a shift known as urbanization.

## A Nation of Immigrants

Industry needed workers, and millions of immigrants came to fill the need and to seek a better life. Newcomers from Europe, Asia, and elsewhere passed through ports such as Ellis Island in New York Harbor, greeted by the Statue of Liberty — a gift from France that became a symbol of welcome and freedom. Immigrants built railroads and cities, started businesses, and reshaped American culture. Their story is central to who Americans are.

## The Price of Growth

Rapid growth had a harsh side. Factory work was often dangerous, hours were long, and pay was low, including for child laborers. Crowded cities struggled with poor housing and sanitation, and a few owners held vast wealth while many workers had little. In response, workers organized **labor unions** to bargain for better wages and safer conditions.

## The Progressive Era

In the early 1900s, reformers known as Progressives pushed government to address these problems. They worked to break up monopolies, protect consumers and workers, reduce political corruption, and expand democracy. Reforms of this era included food and drug safety laws, the direct election of senators (17th Amendment), and — won after a long struggle — women's right to vote nationwide through the 19th Amendment in 1920.

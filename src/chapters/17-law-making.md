## From Idea to Bill

Every law starts as an idea. That idea can come from anyone — a citizen, an interest group, a federal agency, or the President — but only a **member of Congress** can formally introduce it as a **bill**. Once introduced in the House or the Senate, the bill is numbered and sent to the committee that handles its subject. Thousands of bills are introduced each session, and only a small fraction will ever become law.

## Committee Work

The committee stage is where most of the real work — and most of the dying — happens. A committee studies the bill, holds **hearings** where experts and the public can testify, amends the text, and votes on whether to send it forward. A bill the committee declines to advance simply stops there. A bill that survives moves to the full chamber for debate and a **floor vote**. In the Senate, debate can be extended by a **filibuster**, which usually requires 60 votes to overcome.

## Both Chambers Must Agree

A bill must pass **both** the House and the Senate in **exactly the same form**. Because each chamber often changes a bill, the two versions frequently differ. When they do, a **conference committee** of members from both chambers works out a single compromise version, which both chambers must then approve. Only after the House and Senate agree on identical text does the bill go to the President.

## President and Courts

The President has choices. Signing the bill makes it law. A **veto** rejects it — but Congress can **override** a veto with a two-thirds vote in each chamber, turning the bill into law without the President's approval. If the President neither signs nor vetoes within ten days while Congress is in session, the bill becomes law automatically. Even after a law takes effect, the courts may later use judicial review to decide whether it is constitutional. At every step, the design forces cooperation among the branches.

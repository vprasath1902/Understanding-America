## A Nation Half-Free

By 1860 the United States was a country half-free and half-slave, and decades of compromise had run out. When Abraham Lincoln was elected president, eleven Southern states seceded — declared they were leaving the Union — to protect slavery. The central cause of the war that followed was slavery and the question of whether it would expand or end.

## The Civil War

The Civil War (1861–1865) was the deadliest war in American history, fought between the Union (the North) and the Confederacy (the South). In 1863, President Lincoln issued the Emancipation Proclamation, declaring enslaved people in the Confederate states to be free and turning the war into a fight for freedom as well as for union. The Union's victory in 1865 preserved the United States as one nation.

## Three Amendments That Remade the Constitution

After the war, three amendments transformed the Constitution. The **13th Amendment** abolished slavery. The **14th Amendment** guaranteed citizenship to all persons born in the United States and promised "equal protection of the laws." The **15th Amendment** said the right to vote could not be denied because of race. Together they are called the Reconstruction Amendments.

## Reconstruction and Its End

During Reconstruction, the federal government worked to rebuild the South and protect the rights of formerly enslaved people, who voted and held office for the first time. But Reconstruction ended in 1877, and over the following decades many of its protections were stripped away by discriminatory laws and violence. The promises of the 14th and 15th Amendments would not be fully enforced until the Civil Rights Movement nearly a century later.

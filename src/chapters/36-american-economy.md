## A Free-Market Economy

When the civics test asks, "What is the economic system of the United States?", the answer is **capitalism** — also called a **free market economy**. In this system, businesses and individuals — not the government — own most property and decide what to make, buy, and sell. People are free to start companies, choose jobs, and spend their money as they wish.

## Supply and Demand

In a free market, prices and production are guided mostly by **supply and demand** rather than set by the government. When many people want something that is scarce, its price tends to rise; when something is plentiful or less wanted, its price tends to fall. These signals help decide what gets produced and at what price, with competition encouraging better products and lower prices.

## The Government's Role

A free market does not mean no government. The federal government sets **rules** to keep markets fair and safe — protecting consumers and workers, enforcing contracts, and preventing fraud and monopolies. The **Federal Reserve**, the nation's central bank, manages the money supply and interest rates to keep the economy stable. Government also builds infrastructure and provides services that markets alone may not.

## Taxes, Money, and Daily Life

The economy connects directly to citizens through **money** and **taxes**. The federal government coins money and issues currency. It collects **federal taxes** — the income tax was authorized by the **16th Amendment** — to fund the **federal budget**, which pays for national defense, Social Security, roads, and the rest of the government's work. Paying taxes is both required by law and a basic civic responsibility, and understanding the economy helps citizens make informed choices as workers, consumers, and voters.

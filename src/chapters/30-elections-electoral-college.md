## The American Election System

Unlike many countries, the United States does not hold just one national election. Elections happen at the **federal, state, and local** levels, and each chooses leaders for a different part of government. Federal elections fill the Presidency, the Senate, and the House. State elections fill offices like governor and the state legislature. Local elections fill offices like mayor, city council, sheriff, and school board.

## Presidential Elections

The presidential election chooses the **President and Vice President**. It is held every **four years**, on the Tuesday after the first Monday in November (for example, 2024, 2028, 2032). A President serves a four-year term and may serve a maximum of two terms.

## Congressional Elections

Congress has two chambers, elected separately and on different clocks.

- **House of Representatives:** every member serves a **2-year** term, so **all 435 seats** are up for election every two years. This lets voters change the direction of the House quickly.
- **Senate:** senators serve **6-year** terms, but they are divided into three groups ("classes") so that only **about one-third** of the Senate is elected every two years. This keeps experienced members in office for continuity.

## Midterm Elections

**Midterm elections** happen in the middle of a President's four-year term (for example, 2026 falls between the 2024 and 2028 presidential elections). In a midterm, voters elect all House members, about one-third of the Senate, many governors, state legislatures, and local offices. Midterms often decide whether the President's party keeps control of Congress.

## State and Local Elections

Each state elects its own leaders. In Pennsylvania, for example, voters elect the Governor, Lieutenant Governor, Attorney General, State Treasurer, state senators and representatives, and some judges — officials who run the state, not the federal government.

Local elections fill offices such as mayor, county commissioners, sheriff, district attorney, and school board. These offices shape everyday life: schools, roads, police, libraries, parks, and local taxes.

## Primary and General Elections

The November election is not the first step. First, each political party chooses its candidate in a **primary election**, where party members (or, in some states, all voters) decide who will represent the party. Then, in the **general election** in November, voters choose among the parties' nominees — for example, the Republican nominee, the Democratic nominee, and any independent candidates.

## The Presidential Election Cycle

A typical presidential cycle runs: candidates announce campaigns &rarr; primary elections &rarr; party national conventions &rarr; the general election in November &rarr; the Electoral College casts its votes &rarr; Congress counts the electoral votes &rarr; the President is inaugurated on January 20.

## What Is the Electoral College?

Americans do not directly elect the President. Instead, voters in each state elect **electors**, who then formally elect the President. This group of electors is the **Electoral College**.

It was a compromise written into the Constitution in 1787, when the country was large and news traveled slowly. The Founders wanted both the people and the states to have a role, so they chose a middle path between election by Congress and direct election by the people.

## How Electoral Votes Are Counted

Each state's number of electoral votes equals its number of **Representatives plus its two Senators**. Because every state has two senators and at least one representative, every state has **at least 3** electoral votes. Washington, D.C. also has 3.

There are **538** electoral votes in total. To win, a candidate must receive a majority — **270**. In almost every state, the candidate who wins that state's popular vote receives **all** of that state's electoral votes, and a candidate keeps adding states until reaching 270.

## Why It Matters — and the Debate

The Electoral College gives both population and states a role: without it, candidates might campaign almost entirely in the largest cities, and smaller states could be ignored. It encourages building support across many different states.

Critics point out that it can produce a President who did not win the nationwide **popular vote**. Supporters argue it preserves the role of the states and rewards geographically broad coalitions. Both views are part of an ongoing public debate.

## Summary of Election Types

| Election | Purpose | Frequency |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Presidential | Elect the President and Vice President | Every 4 years |
| House of Representatives | Elect all 435 Representatives | Every 2 years |
| Senate | Elect about one-third of senators | Every 2 years |
| Midterm | Congressional, state, and many local offices | Every 2 years (between presidential elections) |
| Governor | Elect the state governor | Usually every 4 years (varies by state) |
| State legislature | Elect state lawmakers | Usually every 2 or 4 years |
| Local | Elect local officials | Varies by jurisdiction |
| Primary | Choose each party's nominee | Before the general election |
| General | Choose among party nominees | Election Day (November) |

## Before the Interview

Once USCIS finishes its initial review, it mails an **interview notice** with the date, time, and location, and a list of documents to bring — typically your green card, identification, and possibly travel and tax records. The best preparation is to review your own **Form N-400** so your answers match what you filed, refresh your **travel history**, and practice the civics questions. Arriving early and well-organized makes the day far less stressful.

## At the Office

At the office you pass through security and wait to be called. When the officer greets you, you take an **oath to tell the truth**. The officer then verifies your identity and goes through your N-400 with you, confirming your answers and asking about anything that needs clarification. This conversation itself is part of the test — it shows the officer that you can speak and understand English.

## The English and Civics Tests

Unless you qualify for an exemption, the officer administers two tests. The **English test** has three parts: speaking (judged during the interview), reading one sentence correctly out of up to three, and writing one sentence correctly out of up to three. The **civics test** is oral: the officer asks up to **10 questions** from the official list of 100, and you must answer **6 correctly** to pass. The previous four chapters cover that civics material.

## Decision and Oath

At the end, the officer records one of three outcomes: **recommended approval**, **continued** (more information or a re-test is needed), or **denied**. Most well-prepared applicants are approved. Approval is followed by the **Oath of Allegiance** ceremony — sometimes the same day, often scheduled later — where you officially become a U.S. citizen and receive your Certificate of Naturalization.

## The People's House

The House of Representatives is often called "the people's house" because it is meant to mirror the population. It has **435 voting members**, and those seats are divided among the states by population: more populous states have more representatives, while every state is guaranteed at least one. After each ten-year **census**, the seats are re-divided — a process called **apportionment** — and district lines may be redrawn to reflect where people now live.

## Districts and Two-Year Terms

Each representative is elected from a single **congressional district** and serves a **two-year term**. Those short terms are intentional: they force representatives to face the voters often, keeping them responsive to changing public opinion. Because the whole House stands for election every two years, the chamber can shift direction quickly when voters want change. Members must be at least 25 years old, U.S. citizens for seven years, and live in the state they represent.

## Leadership and Special Powers

The House chooses a **Speaker of the House**, who leads the chamber, sets much of its agenda, and is second in line to the presidency after the Vice President. The House also holds two powers the Senate does not: **revenue bills** — proposals to raise taxes — must begin in the House, and the House alone can **impeach** federal officials, meaning it brings the formal charges that the Senate then tries.

## Your District

Every voter lives in one congressional district and is represented by one member of the House. Your district depends on your home address, and it can change after each census. You can find your current representative at [house.gov](https://www.house.gov/representatives/find-your-representative), and Appendix H lists current officeholders. Knowing your representative is the first step to making your voice heard in Congress.

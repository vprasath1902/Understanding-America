## The Cold War

After World War II, the United States and the **Soviet Union** became rivals in a decades-long struggle called the Cold War. It was "cold" because the two superpowers never fought each other directly, but it shaped global politics, the arms race, and the space race — which produced the first Moon landing in 1969. The main American concern throughout was the spread of **communism**.

To contain communism, the United States did fight two major "hot" wars during this period: the **Korean War** (1950–1953) and the **Vietnam War**. In both, the U.S. goal was to stop the spread of communism. The Cold War also drove a costly nuclear arms race that shadowed the era.

## The Civil Rights Movement

At home, Black Americans and their allies organized to end **segregation** — the legal separation of people by race — and to secure the voting rights promised long before by the 14th and 15th Amendments. In 1954, the Supreme Court ruled in *Brown v. Board of Education* that segregated public schools were unconstitutional. Leaders such as Martin Luther King Jr. and Rosa Parks inspired peaceful protest. The movement won landmark laws: the **Civil Rights Act of 1964**, banning discrimination, and the **Voting Rights Act of 1965**, protecting the right to vote.

## A Changing Society

The same decades brought broader change. Movements for women's rights, the rights of people with disabilities, and other groups expanded the meaning of equality. Immigration reform in 1965 reopened the country to newcomers from around the world, making America more diverse than ever.

## The End of the Cold War and a New Century

The Cold War ended around **1991** with the collapse of the Soviet Union, leaving the United States as the world's leading power. Shortly before, in the **Persian Gulf War** (1991), the U.S. led a coalition to force the Iraqi military out of Kuwait.

Then, on **September 11, 2001**, terrorists hijacked four airplanes and attacked the United States, crashing two into the World Trade Center in New York City, one into the Pentagon, and one into a field in Pennsylvania. Nearly 3,000 people died. In response, the United States launched the **War on Terror**, including the **War in Afghanistan** and the **War in Iraq**.

## Modern America

Since then, technology — computers, the internet, and smartphones — has transformed how Americans work, learn, and connect, and in 2008 the country elected its first Black president. The nation continues to debate its direction, but it does so through the same constitutional system the founders created and the same ongoing effort to live up to the promise of liberty and equality for all.

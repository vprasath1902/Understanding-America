## The Cold War

After World War II, the United States and the Soviet Union became rivals in a decades-long struggle called the Cold War. It was "cold" because the two superpowers never fought each other directly, but it shaped global politics, the arms race, the space race, and conflicts around the world. The main American concern throughout was the spread of **communism**. The race to space produced milestones such as the first Moon landing in 1969.

## The Civil Rights Movement

At home, Black Americans and their allies organized to end **segregation** — the legal separation of people by race — and to secure the voting rights promised long before by the 14th and 15th Amendments. In 1954, the Supreme Court ruled in *Brown v. Board of Education* that segregated public schools were unconstitutional. Leaders such as Martin Luther King Jr. and Rosa Parks inspired peaceful protest. The movement won landmark laws: the **Civil Rights Act of 1964**, banning discrimination, and the **Voting Rights Act of 1965**, protecting the right to vote.

## A Changing Society

The same decades brought broader change. Movements for women's rights, the rights of people with disabilities, and other groups expanded the meaning of equality. Immigration reform in 1965 reopened the country to newcomers from around the world, making America more diverse than ever.

## Modern America

The Cold War ended around 1991 with the collapse of the Soviet Union, leaving the United States as the world's leading power. Since then, technology — computers, the internet, and smartphones — has transformed how Americans work, learn, and connect. The nation continues to debate its direction, but it does so through the same constitutional system the founders created and the same ongoing effort to live up to the promise of liberty and equality for all.

## Your Government Tree

Government can feel abstract until you place yourself in it. From your own home, you are connected upward through several layers: your city or township, then your county, then your state, and finally the United States. Each layer is a real government with its own officials, budget, and responsibilities — and you live under all of them at once. Picturing this "government tree" turns a confusing system into something concrete.

## Your Representatives

Wherever you live, people have been elected to represent you at every level. For the nation, you have a U.S. Representative for your district and two U.S. Senators for your state. For the state, you have a Governor and state legislators. Closer to home, you have county officials, city or township officials, and school board members. As a **constituent**, you can contact any of them — their job is to represent you.

## Who Do I Contact?

Knowing which office handles what saves time and frustration. Citizenship and green cards go to **USCIS**; passports to the U.S. Department of State; driver licenses to your state's motor vehicle agency (often called the DMV); federal taxes to the IRS; and Social Security to the SSA. Local matters — trash, water, local roads, building permits — go to city, township, or county offices. For a worked example showing one state in detail, see **Appendix E: Pennsylvania Citizen's Guide**.

## Why Local Government Matters

National politics gets the most attention, but local government often shapes daily life the most. Your **school board** sets school policy and budgets; your township or city handles roads, zoning, parks, and police; your county runs elections and keeps property records. These decisions are made close to home, by officials you can meet — and local elections are often decided by very few votes, so each one carries real weight.

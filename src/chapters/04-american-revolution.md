## A War Few Expected America to Win

On paper, the colonies had little chance. Britain possessed a professional army, the world's most powerful navy, deep financial resources, and a global empire. The Americans had a small, poorly supplied army, little money, and a weak central government that struggled to coordinate the states. What they did have was determination, the advantage of fighting on home ground, and a cause that drew broad popular support.

## George Washington's Leadership

Command of the **Continental Army** fell to **George Washington**, and his leadership proved decisive. Washington grasped a crucial point: he did not need to defeat Britain outright: he only needed to keep his army intact and the cause alive until Britain tired of the war's cost. Through repeated setbacks, his steadiness, integrity, and refusal to seek personal power made him a unifying national symbol — later earning him the title "Father of Our Country."

## Turning Points

Two moments stand out. The American victory at **Saratoga** in 1777 convinced **France** that the Americans could win, and France entered the war as an ally, providing money, troops, and a navy that changed the balance. The brutal winter at **Valley Forge** tested and ultimately hardened the army. Finally, in 1781, American and French forces trapped a British army at **Yorktown**, forcing a surrender that effectively ended major fighting.

## Independence Recognized

The **Treaty of Paris**, signed in 1783, formally ended the war and recognized the **independence of the United States**, with generous boundaries stretching west to the Mississippi River. The improbable had happened: thirteen colonies had won their freedom. But independence brought a new and difficult challenge — the former colonies now had to figure out how to govern themselves, the subject of the next chapter.

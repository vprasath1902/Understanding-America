## Birth and School

A citizen's relationship with government begins at birth. **Vital records** — a birth certificate — are issued by state and local governments and become the foundation for nearly every later document. In childhood, **public education** is provided and funded mostly by states and local school districts, while federal agencies add a Social Security number, civil-rights protections, and some education funding. From the very start, all three levels of government are present.

## Driving, Work, and Taxes

As young people grow, government keeps pace. A teenager gets a driver license from the **state** motor vehicle agency. A first job brings a paycheck with federal and state taxes withheld, reported to the **IRS** and the **Social Security Administration**. Workplace safety, minimum-wage, and anti-discrimination rules — set by labor agencies — protect workers throughout their careers. Each of these everyday milestones runs through a public system.

## Home and Family

Building a life adds new touchpoints. Marriage is recorded by the county or state; buying a home involves property records, local **property taxes**, and zoning rules; raising children connects families back to schools and health programs. These matters are handled largely by **county, township, and state** offices — the levels of government closest to home.

## Retirement and Civic Life

In later years, federal programs such as **Social Security** and **Medicare** provide retirement income and health coverage earned over a lifetime of work. But the journey is not only about receiving services. Throughout life, citizens give back — they **vote**, answer the call for **jury service**, volunteer, attend local meetings, and help their communities. That ongoing **civic participation** is what citizenship looks like in practice: a lifelong, two-way relationship with the constitutional system this book has described.

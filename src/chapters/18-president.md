## Why the Presidency Exists

The founders wanted leadership that could act quickly and speak for the whole nation, but they feared creating a new king. Their answer was a single elected President with real but **limited** power. The President is elected to a four-year term through the Electoral College, must be at least 35 years old and a natural-born citizen, and — under the **Twenty-Second Amendment** — may serve no more than two terms. The office combines several roles: head of government, head of state, and Commander in Chief of the armed forces.

## Powers

The President's powers are substantial. The President signs bills into law or **vetoes** them; appoints Cabinet officers, ambassadors, and federal judges (with Senate confirmation); and directs the executive departments and agencies. As **Commander in Chief**, the President leads the military, though only Congress can declare war and fund it. The President conducts foreign relations and negotiates treaties, issues **executive orders** to direct how laws are carried out, and can grant **pardons** for federal crimes.

## Limits

Just as important is what the President cannot do. The President **cannot make laws** alone — only Congress can. The President cannot **raise taxes** or **spend money** that Congress has not approved, cannot ignore the Constitution or a court ruling, and cannot control how Congress votes or how judges decide. Presidents who overstep can be checked by Congress, the courts, and ultimately the voters; serious misconduct can lead to impeachment and removal. These limits are what separate a President from a monarch.

## Vice President and Cabinet

The President does not work alone. The **Vice President** is first in line to become President if the President dies, resigns, or cannot serve, and casts the tie-breaking vote in the Senate. The **Cabinet** — the heads of the executive departments — advises the President and runs the day-to-day work of government, a structure explored in the next chapters.

## Three Colonial Regions

By the 1700s, thirteen British colonies lined the Atlantic coast, and historians usually group them into three regions: **New England**, the **Middle** colonies, and the **Southern** colonies. They shared a language and a king, but geography, climate, religion, and economy made daily life in each region strikingly different — differences that would later shape American politics.

## New England Colonies

The New England colonies — Massachusetts, New Hampshire, Rhode Island, and Connecticut — had rocky soil and cold winters that discouraged large farms. Instead, New Englanders turned to fishing, shipbuilding, and trade. Their tight-knit communities, often built around Puritan congregations, governed themselves through **town meetings**, an early and direct form of self-government.

## Middle Colonies

The Middle colonies — New York, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, and Delaware — enjoyed fertile farmland and navigable rivers, becoming known for grain and trade. They were also the most diverse, drawing settlers of many backgrounds and faiths. **Pennsylvania**, founded by William Penn as a haven for Quakers, became famous for its **religious tolerance** and represented self-government.

## Southern Colonies

The Southern colonies — Maryland, Virginia, North Carolina, South Carolina, and Georgia — had warm climates and long growing seasons suited to cash crops like tobacco, rice, and indigo, often grown on large **plantations**. That economy came to depend heavily on the forced labor of **enslaved Africans** — a profound injustice woven into the colonies' growth and one the new nation would not confront for generations. Despite these deep regional differences, all thirteen colonies shared the practice of electing representatives to local assemblies, a common thread that helped them eventually unite.

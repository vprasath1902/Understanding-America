## Branches and the Rule of Law

The federal government has **three branches**: the **legislative** branch (Congress) makes the laws, the **executive** branch (the President) carries them out, and the **judicial** branch (the courts) interprets them. The branches share power so that none can dominate — the system of **checks and balances**. Underlying it all is the **rule of law**: everyone, including the President and members of Congress, must obey the law.

## Congress

Congress is the legislative branch, and it has two parts: the **Senate** and the **House of Representatives**. There are **100 senators** — two from each state — and **435 voting members** of the House, divided among the states by population. Senators serve six-year terms and representatives two-year terms. We elect a U.S. senator for our state and a U.S. representative for our district.

## The President

The **President** leads the executive branch, signs bills into law or **vetoes** them, and serves as **Commander in Chief** of the military. The President is elected to a four-year term. If the President can no longer serve, the **Vice President** becomes President. The Cabinet advises the President.

## The Courts

The **judicial** branch reviews laws, explains laws, resolves disputes, and decides whether a law follows the Constitution. The highest court is the **Supreme Court**, which today has nine justices. Its power to strike down laws that violate the Constitution — judicial review — is the courts' strongest check on the other branches.

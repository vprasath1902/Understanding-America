## Why Courts Exist

Laws are written in general terms, and people disagree about what they mean and how they apply. The **judicial branch** exists to settle those disputes fairly and independently. Federal courts hear cases ranging from disagreements between states to challenges against acts of Congress, and they decide what the law — and ultimately the Constitution — requires. By resolving conflicts through reasoned decisions rather than force, the courts protect both individual rights and the rule of law.

## Court Structure

The federal system has three main levels. Most cases begin in the **District Courts**, the trial courts where evidence is presented and facts are decided. A party who loses may appeal to a **Court of Appeals**, which reviews whether the law was applied correctly. At the top sits the **Supreme Court**, with nine justices, which has the final word on questions of federal law and the Constitution. The Supreme Court hears only a small number of cases each year, usually those of the greatest national importance, and its decisions set **precedent** that guides all lower courts.

## Judicial Review

The judiciary's most powerful tool is **judicial review** — the authority to declare a law or government action unconstitutional and therefore void. This power is not spelled out in the Constitution; the Supreme Court established it in the 1803 case **Marbury v. Madison**. Judicial review is what allows the courts to check Congress and the President, ensuring that even popular laws cannot override the Constitution.

## Independence

To decide cases honestly, judges must be free from political pressure. Federal judges are nominated by the President and confirmed by the Senate, and they serve "during good behavior" — effectively for life — and their pay cannot be cut. Because they never have to win an election or please a party to keep their jobs, they can rule based on the law even when their decisions are unpopular. This **independence** is a cornerstone of equal justice.

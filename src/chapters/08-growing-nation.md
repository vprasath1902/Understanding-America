## Doubling the Country

In 1803, President Thomas Jefferson bought the vast Louisiana Territory from France, roughly doubling the size of the United States overnight. To learn what lay west, Jefferson sent Meriwether Lewis and William Clark on an expedition to the Pacific, guided in part by the Shoshone interpreter Sacagawea. Over the following decades the country pushed steadily westward, and many Americans came to believe in "Manifest Destiny" — the idea that the nation was meant to stretch from the Atlantic to the Pacific.

## The Cost of Expansion

Expansion was not into empty land. Native American nations had lived across the continent for thousands of years, and they were pushed off their homelands as settlers moved west. The Indian Removal Act led to the forced march of the Cherokee and other nations along what became known as the Trail of Tears, on which thousands died. Expansion brought opportunity for some Americans and devastation for others.

## Slavery and a Dividing Nation

In the South, the cotton economy made slavery more entrenched and more profitable. As new territories sought statehood, the question of whether they would allow slavery turned every expansion into a political crisis. Compromises in Congress repeatedly delayed the conflict but never resolved it, and the divide between free and slave states — "sectionalism" — deepened.

## A Spirit of Reform

The same era saw Americans organize to improve their society. The abolition movement, led by figures such as Frederick Douglass, worked to end slavery. The movement for women's rights gathered at the 1848 Seneca Falls Convention, where reformers including Elizabeth Cady Stanton demanded equal rights, including the vote. These movements planted ideas that would shape the nation for the next century.

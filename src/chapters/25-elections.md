## Who Can Vote

Voting in U.S. federal elections is a right reserved for **citizens**. To cast a ballot, a person must generally be a U.S. citizen, at least 18 years old by Election Day, and a resident of the state where they vote. Lawful permanent residents (green card holders) and other non-citizens **cannot** vote in federal elections. For new citizens, registering and voting for the first time is one of the most meaningful steps of full membership in the nation.

## Registering to Vote

In most states, citizens must **register** before they can vote, providing basic information so officials can confirm eligibility and assign the correct ballot. Registration rules and deadlines vary by state, and some states offer same-day registration. You can start at **vote.gov**, which links to every state's registration system. Once registered, you stay on the rolls unless you move or fail to vote for a long period, though rules differ by state.

## Primaries, General, and Midterm Elections

American elections come in several kinds. In a **primary election**, members of a party (or, in some states, all voters) choose the party's nominee. In the **general election** each November, voters choose among those nominees and any independent candidates to fill the office. Presidential elections happen every four years; the **midterm elections** fall in between and decide all House seats, about a third of the Senate, and many state and local offices — often shifting the balance of power in Congress.

## Many Offices

Voting is not just about the President. On a typical ballot, voters choose U.S. senators and representatives, a governor and state legislators, county and city or township officials, school board members, and in many places judges and ballot questions. Local offices, decided by relatively few votes, can affect daily life the most. The President is chosen in a special way — through the **Electoral College** — which the next-to-last chapter, *Elections and the Electoral College*, explains in full.

## The Constitution at a Glance

Written in 1787 and in force since 1789, the Constitution is the **supreme law of the land** and the oldest written national constitution still in use. It is remarkably short. It opens with a **Preamble** stating its purposes, followed by **seven Articles** that build the government, and **twenty-seven amendments** added over more than two centuries. In a few pages, it both **creates** the government and **limits** what that government may do.

## We the People

The Preamble begins with three famous words: "**We the People**." They announce a revolutionary principle — that government draws its authority from the people themselves, not from a monarch. This idea is called **popular sovereignty**. Everything that follows, from elections to the courts, rests on the consent of the governed.

## The Seven Articles

The body of the Constitution is organized into seven **Articles**. **Article I** creates Congress, the lawmaking branch; **Article II** creates the Presidency; and **Article III** creates the federal courts. **Article IV** describes the relationships among the states; **Article V** explains how to amend the Constitution; **Article VI** contains the **Supremacy Clause**, making the Constitution and federal law the highest law; and **Article VII** set out how the document would be ratified. Together they form the structure of the entire federal government.

## How the Constitution Is Amended

The founders knew they could not foresee everything, so **Article V** provides a way to **amend** the Constitution. The usual path requires a **two-thirds vote of both houses of Congress** to propose an amendment, followed by **ratification by three-fourths of the states** (38 of 50 today). That high bar is deliberate: it lets the country make lasting change when there is broad agreement, while protecting the Constitution from passing political moods. In more than 230 years, only **27** amendments have been adopted out of thousands proposed.

## The Twenty-Seven Amendments

The amendments fall into a few clear groups. (Appendix C lists all 27 with dates.)

- **The Bill of Rights (1–10, 1791).** The first ten amendments protect core liberties — speech, religion, the press, assembly, fair trials, and powers reserved to the states and people. They are covered in detail in the next chapter.
- **The Civil War Amendments (13, 14, 15).** Ratified between 1865 and 1870, they **abolished slavery** (13th), guaranteed **citizenship and equal protection** (14th), and barred denying the vote based on **race** (15th) — remaking the Constitution after the Civil War.
- **Expanding democracy.** Several amendments widened who governs and who votes: the **17th** gave voters (not legislatures) the power to elect senators; the **19th** secured women's right to vote; the **23rd** gave Washington, D.C. electoral votes; the **24th** banned poll taxes; and the **26th** set the voting age at 18.
- **Tuning the machinery of government.** Others adjusted how government runs: the **12th** fixed how electors vote for President and Vice President; the **20th** set terms of office; the **22nd** limited the President to two terms; the **25th** clarified presidential succession and disability; and the **27th** governs congressional pay.
- **Other changes.** The **11th** limited certain lawsuits against states, and the **16th** authorized a federal income tax. The **18th** established Prohibition — and the **21st** repealed it, the only amendment to undo another.

## Why It Endures

The pattern across the amendments tells a story: the Constitution has slowly become **more inclusive**, extending citizenship and the vote to people the original document left out. By stating durable principles while allowing careful change, the framework the founders built in 1787 has lasted — not unchanged, but capable of growing toward its own promise of liberty and equality for all.

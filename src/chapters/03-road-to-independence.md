## After the French and Indian War

Britain defeated France in the French and Indian War in 1763, gaining vast new territory — but also enormous debt. Parliament decided the American colonies should help pay for their own defense and began imposing new taxes, including the **Sugar Act** and the **Stamp Act**, which taxed printed materials. To the colonists, taxes imposed from London felt like a sharp break from the self-rule they were used to.

## No Taxation Without Representation

The colonists' core objection was constitutional, not merely financial. They argued that since they had **no elected representatives in Parliament**, Parliament had no right to tax them — "**No Taxation Without Representation**." The principle at stake was **consent**: that a free people should be governed only with their agreement. This idea would later sit at the heart of American government.

## Protest and Escalation

Resistance grew step by step. Colonists boycotted British goods; tensions turned deadly in the 1770 **Boston Massacre**; and in 1773 the **Boston Tea Party** dumped British tea into the harbor in protest of the Tea Act. Britain responded with harsh laws the colonists called the **Intolerable Acts**. In response, delegates from the colonies began meeting as the **Continental Congress** to coordinate a united response.

## Toward War

In April 1775, British troops and colonial militia clashed at **Lexington and Concord**, the "shot heard round the world" that began the Revolutionary War. For over a year the colonies fought while still debating whether to seek full independence. Then, on **July 4, 1776**, the Continental Congress adopted the **Declaration of Independence**, formally announcing that the colonies were free states. Declaring independence, however, was only the beginning — it still had to be won on the battlefield.

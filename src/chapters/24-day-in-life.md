## Morning Systems

An ordinary day is full of government before you ever leave the house. The water from the tap meets safety standards set by federal and state rules and is delivered by a local utility. The electricity is carried over a regulated power grid. The cereal and milk you eat were inspected under federal food-safety laws. The weather report relies on the National Weather Service. None of this is visible, but all of it is the quiet work of public systems.

## Work and School

The rest of the day continues the pattern. Workplaces follow safety and wage rules; paychecks have federal and state taxes withheld; children attend schools funded and regulated mostly by states and localities. Roads, traffic signals, and public transit are built and maintained by state and local governments. Even the internet you use travels over **infrastructure** shaped by public policy. Government rarely announces itself, but it is woven through work and school.

## Travel and Services

Travel makes the role of government especially clear. Mail is delivered by the U.S. Postal Service; a passport comes from the Department of State; airport screening is run by the TSA; interstate highways were built with federal funding; and national and state parks are managed by public agencies. When you drive, your license and the rules of the road come from your state. Behind each convenience stands an **agency** doing its job.

## Rights Throughout the Day

All day long, the Constitution protects you whether or not you think about it. Your freedom to speak, worship, and read what you choose; your privacy against unreasonable searches; **due process** if you deal with the legal system; and fair, equal treatment under the law — these protections are always in force. The genius of the system is that rights and services operate in the background, letting citizens simply live their lives.
